Highlights

  • Key features:

    How does Priorities differ from traditional performance management processes?

    • Recognizes project/team responsibilities as well as functional responsibilities

    • Accommodates multiple inputs and variable cycle times

    • Promotes frequent reevaluation of high priority work

    • Is flexible; reduces emphasis on completing forms

    • Establishes performance management as a way of work -- not an event

    • Focuses on ongoing dialogue - there are no rating codes

    • Measures behaviors and results (outcomes)

The Priorities process

Priorities is a real-time process you use to set performance expectations with your manager/leader.

You'll use the Priorities process and tools to:

  • Clarify your role and key responsibilities for your ongoing work and for major projects

  • Set expectations on behaviors that will support your team’s performance

  • Agree on deliverables and results expected for your ongoing work and for major projects

  • Evaluate your results and behaviors

  • Create a personal portfolio to showcase your achievements in preparation for your next career step

The heart of Priorities: four principles

Prioritiesis not an annual or forms-driven process, but a way of working in Nortel Networks as you focus on four key principles:

  • You understand what your role is, how it adds value to the business and our customers, and what your priorities are

  • You understand the outcomes and behaviors expected of you, and are responsive and flexible when priorities change

  • You give and receive feedback continuously, ensuring that customers and colleagues are getting the quality they need and that your individual and team performance is improving day-to-day

  • You know what you want to do at Nortel Networks and are “marketing” yourself to achieve that goal

Employees’ role and responsibilities

Understand and apply the four principles.

  • Initiate dialogue with their functional or project manager at any time, formally or informally, to set expectations and get feedback on progress.

  • Seek feedback at any time from sources other than own managers/leaders, mentors or customers as appropriate.

Maintain any Priorities tools used, other than the Performance Evaluation Summary.

Functional managers'/leaders' role and responsibilities

Every employee has a functional manager/leader who maintains an ongoing relationship regardless of project assignments and who:

  • Sets functional expectations

  • Evaluates performance on these expectations

  • Completes a Performance Evaluation Summary (MSWord format / Frame / MSWord A4 format / PDF A4 format) reflecting all the employee's work (input from employee, project managers, others)

    • At least once each year (twice if the employee is eligible for incentive programs e.g., SUCCESS)

  • Forwards the completed Performance Evaluation Summary to Employee Services for inclusion in the employment file.

Project managers’ role and responsibilities

If an employee is assigned to a project team, the project manager:

  • Sets expectations with him or her

  • Provides on-going feedback

  • Evaluates performance against the expectations for that project

  • Completes a Performance Evaluation Summary (MSWord format / Frame / MSWord A4 format / PDF A4 format) to reflect overall performance on that project at the end

  • Provides this input to the functional manager/leader for inclusion in the employee's overall Performance Evaluation Summary for the period.

Evaluating performance

  • You should receive an evaluation of your performance:

    • Whenever you want feedback on how you’re doing

    • From your project manager at the end of your involvement in a project

    • From your functional manager/leader to summarize your overall performance

  • Feedback may be formal or informal

  • Evaluations should incorporate input from as many sources as reasonably possible – customers, peers/team members, managers, leaders and subordinates – as well as your functional manager/leader.

  • All evaluations will consider BOTH required outcomes AND your behaviors/performance dimensions.

Definitions for evaluating outcomes

Missed

Met

Surpassed

The majority of the individual’s "Required Outcomes" on their Performance Expectations missed, and corrective and alternative actions not identified and put in place by the individual.

Typical issues:

  • Required outcomes late or missing

  • Targets or quotas missed

  • Development/training activities not put into practice back on the job, or no agreed performance improvement observed

  • Feedback from customers/clients indicate that their operational requirements are not being met

  • Work needs to be re-done or dealt with by others in order to achieve previously agreed quality or deadlines

All or most of the individual’s "Required Outcomes" on their Performance Expectations achieved, and corrective and alternative actions taken by individual for those that were not achievable.

Typical Achievements:

  • Required outcomes achieved on time and to specification

  • Targets or quotas achieved

  • Development activities put into practice on the job and/or required performance improvement was demonstrated

All or most of the individual’s "Required Outcomes" on their Performance Expectations were achieved more successfully than originally specified, and/or additional milestones were achieved.

Typical Achievements:

  • Takes on and completes work additional to that originally agreed on the performance agreement

  • Completes objectives ahead of time

  • Completes tasks despite losing the agreed resources

  • Overcomes unexpected and severe obstacles

  • Targets or quotas were significantly exceeded

  • Contributes extra value to products or services which increases productivity, profitability, and/or customer/client satisfaction

  • Development activities are turned into value-add on-the-job capability improvements

Note:
Employees evaluated shortly after taking on a new role should be measured against the required outcomes defined on their Performance Expectations for the period being evaluated.

Evaluating behaviors

Below Expectations

As Expected

Beyond Expectations

Observation and feedback indicate that work gets done without sufficient attention to behavioral expectations defined on the Performance Expectations.

Typical observations include:

  • Behavior not in line with those defined in Performance Expectations

  • Behaviors not in line with local or organization or environmental expectations/norms

  • Actions taken without regard for their impact on other individuals, organizations or the company

  • Completion of work achieved with negative impact to others

  • Behavior raises questions of motive and/or purpose

  • Opportunities missed for collaboration that would benefit the "bigger picture"

  • Actions not reflective or contrary to Nortel Networks' Leadership Expectations

  • Knowledge/information held back when needed by others

Observation and feedback indicate that work gets done in line with behavioral expectations defined on the Performance Expectations.

Typical observations include:

  • Actions demonstrate commitment to behaviors defined in the Performance Expectations.

  • Behavior fits and complements organization or environmental expectations or norms

  • Actions and decisions reflect commitment to collaboration

  • Demonstrates Nortel Networks' Leadership Expectations

  • Knowledge and information recorded effectively for later use by the organization

Observation and feedback indicate that work gets done with particular emphasis on "how" things get done, and behavior clearly adds value to the quality and quantity of outcomes

Typical observations include:

  • Actions, decisions and behavior exemplify Nortel Networks' Leadership Expectations

  • Collaborative, supportive action taken whenever possible

  • An exemplar to others for the behaviors defined in the Performance Expectations

  • "Leads by example," encouraging and rewarding others to work toward high quality behavior and performance

  • Knowledge is transferred to people, systems and/or processes and creates performance improvement

Note: Employees evaluated shortly after taking on a new role should be measured against the behaviors/performance dimensions defined on their Performance Expectations for the period being evaluated.

Evaluating mastery of a PD

Nortel Networks' Performance Dimensions provide definitions for 22 behaviors associated with high performance. Each PD has four mastery levels to help you specifically define behavioral expectations. When setting behavioral expectations, look at the Performance Dimension definitions and behavior examples to determine the critical PDs the employee needs to demonstrate in delivering his/her accountabilities. When evaluating mastery of these PDs

  • Consider the employee’s behavior against these

  • Take a broad, full look over the last 10-12 months

  • Make notes to support your opinion (these should include specific examples of behavior)

  • To determine mastery level (1-4), look at what is consistently demonstrated

Giving feedback using the performance matrix

Two dimensions – results and behaviors. Both are vital to performance in a team environment.

Value:

  • Allows a meaningful discussion about the factors driving the evaluation.
  • Avoids labeling with a “rating”.
  • The X can be placed anywhere allowing differentiation within each of the three categories on each axis – and thus should create a qualitative discussion.
  • Managers/leaders can use an employee’s position on this matrix as one source of input to compensation decisions. Compensation decisions are influenced by a number of factors, including performance.

Evaluation - the matrix

As well as giving a written summary of how an employee performed, the person giving feedback can show how the employee did against two dimensions.

Here, there would be no "score" or "performance rating". Instead, a qualitative discussion can take place on what the position means and how to move towards the top right corner.

Evaluation - Define "Top Performance”

The level of performance expected (and rewarded) must be defined - what weight do behaviors have in your organization?

Specifically, is being in the red box ("surpassed" outcomes and "as expected" behaviors) sufficient to be a top performer in a role, project or organization? Or, is being in the yellow box required?

To focus employees’ effort and avoid contradictions, managers/leaders need to ensure employees know (and employees should find out) what level of performance is expected and what will be rewarded.

Evaluation an aid in allocating rewards

We recommend managers/leaders use the individual’s matrix position and ranking to help guide compensation decisions (including determination of the performance component of an employee's salary planning increase, and for eligible employees; the IPF or % incentive payout, and number of stock options)

Evaluation - for comparative analysis

Managers/leaders can map all employees onto a single matrix, where they can be compared/ranked relative to one another.

Being more granular than the old MFA rating, performers to be identified and documented.

Success

  • Success hinges on thinking in terms of the four principles and maintaining a clear understanding of your role, your accountabilities and the behaviors expected as part of your everyday work
  • Filling out the forms will not ensure success
  • Open and on-going dialogue is critical
